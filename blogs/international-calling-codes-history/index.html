The History of International Calling Codes: Why Phone Numbers Start with +91, +1, or +44

Published on 25 February 2026

Today, dialing someone across the world feels almost effortless. You type a number, add a "+" symbol, and within seconds your call travels across continents.

But the system behind those simple digits — numbers like +91 for India, +1 for the United States, or +44 for the United Kingdom — is the result of decades of technological evolution, global cooperation, and a world slowly learning how to connect.

To understand why countries have unique international calling codes, we need to go back to the early days of telephones — a time when even calling the next town was an impressive technological achievement.

When Phone Calls Were Manual

In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, telephones were still a new invention. After the pioneering work of Alexander Graham Bell, telephone networks began appearing in cities across Europe and North America.

But making a phone call was nothing like it is today.

If you wanted to call someone, you would lift the receiver and speak to a human operator at a switchboard. The operator manually connected wires to route your call to the correct line.

Instead of dialing numbers, people often used names or simple identifiers, such as:

  • “Connect me to Market Street 23”
  • “Call Central Office 5”

Long-distance calls were even more complicated. Operators had to coordinate with multiple telephone exchanges along the route. In some cases, several operators worked together to connect a single call between cities.

This system worked — but only while telephone networks were relatively small.

The Rapid Growth of Telephone Networks

By the 1930s and 1940s, telephone usage had exploded. Cities had thousands of lines, and entire countries had millions of active telephone connections.

The manual system began to struggle under the growing demand.

Three major challenges emerged:

  • Too many phone numbers to manage
  • Confusion between different national telephone networks
  • International calls becoming extremely difficult to coordinate

At that time, each country had built its telephone system independently. There was no global structure or standardized numbering plan guiding how phone numbers should be organized.

If the world wanted a truly global communication network, it needed a universal system.

The Birth of International Calling Codes

In the 1960s, the international telecommunications community stepped in to solve the problem.

The International Telecommunication Union (ITU) introduced a system called International Direct Dialling (IDD).

This system is based on what is now known as the E.164 international numbering standard, which created a structured way to dial phone numbers across countries.

The world was divided into numbering zones, and every country was assigned a unique international country calling code.

These are the prefixes we see today before international phone numbers.

  • +1 — United States, Canada, and parts of the Caribbean
  • +44 — United Kingdom
  • +61 — Australia
  • +81 — Japan
  • +91 — India

The "+" symbol simply indicates that the caller should dial their country's international access code first.

For example:

  • From India, the international prefix is 00
  • From the United States, it is 011

Modern smartphones automatically interpret the "+" symbol, making international dialing much easier.

Why Some Countries Have Shorter Calling Codes

You may have noticed that some countries have shorter country calling codes, while others have longer ones.

This was not random. When the numbering system was designed, larger or earlier telephone networks were often given shorter codes because they handled higher call volumes.

The United States and Canada share the code +1, forming what is known as the North American Numbering Plan.

Other regions were assigned codes beginning with different digits based on geographic zones.

This structured system made global telecommunications far easier to manage and allowed networks from different countries to connect seamlessly.

The System That Connects the World

Today, international calling codes are so integrated into modern communication that most people never think about them.

Whether you are making a business call, contacting family overseas, or verifying a phone number online, those few digits at the beginning of a phone number quietly direct your call through the global telecommunications network.

Behind every "+91" or "+44" is more than a number — it is part of the infrastructure that allows billions of people to communicate across borders every day.

Final Thoughts

The international calling code system is one of the most successful examples of global technological cooperation.

Built decades ago, it continues to support modern smartphones, internet-based calling services, and global business communication.

The next time you dial a number that begins with "+", remember that those digits represent a carefully designed system that helped transform telephones from local devices into a truly global network.
